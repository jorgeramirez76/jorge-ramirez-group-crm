# 5 NYC Commuter Towns in NJ That Investors Are Sleeping On

**By Jorge Ramirez | The Jorge Ramirez Group, Keller Williams Realty Premier Properties**

---

Everyone on this forum knows the Jersey City and Hoboken play. Waterfront condos, PATH train access, young professionals paying Manhattan-adjacent rents. That ship sailed years ago — cap rates are compressed, prices are fully baked, and you're competing with institutional money on every decent listing.

But 15-30 minutes further west on NJ Transit, there are five towns where the fundamentals are strong, the rental demand is real, and most investors aren't paying attention yet. I've sold over 500 homes across Northern New Jersey in the past 15+ years, and these are the markets where I see the best risk-adjusted opportunity for buy-and-hold investors right now.

## 1. Summit

**The Basics:**
- Commute to NYC Penn Station: 45-50 minutes (NJ Transit Midtown Direct)
- Median home price: ~$950,000
- Average 2BR rental: $2,800-$3,400/month
- School district rating: Top 10 in the state

**Why Investors Should Care:**

Summit flies under the radar because of its high entry point, but that's exactly what creates the opportunity. The town's strict zoning and limited buildable land constrain supply in a way that protects long-term appreciation. Downtown Summit has undergone a quiet renaissance — new restaurants, a renovated train station area, and walkability that rivals towns twice its size.

The real play here is multi-family. Summit has a stock of well-maintained 2-4 unit properties near the downtown corridor. Rents are supported by a tenant base that includes young professionals who want the town's quality of life but aren't ready to buy, plus empty nesters downsizing from single-family homes who want to stay in the community.

Appreciation in Summit has consistently outpaced the broader Essex/Union County market. The Midtown Direct train line is a structural advantage that isn't going away.

## 2. Cranford

**The Basics:**
- Commute to NYC Penn Station: 55-65 minutes (NJ Transit Raritan Valley Line)
- Median home price: ~$625,000
- Average 2BR rental: $2,200-$2,700/month
- School district rating: Strong, consistently ranked in Union County's top tier

**Why Investors Should Care:**

Cranford is where I'd point any investor who wants a lower entry point with genuine upside. The town has a charming, walkable downtown centered around a river park, and it's attracted a wave of younger families priced out of Summit and Westfield.

The key metric: Cranford's price-to-rent ratio is more favorable than most of its neighbors. You can acquire a duplex or small multi-family for $600,000-$800,000 and generate rental income that actually covers your carrying costs — something that's nearly impossible in the higher-priced towns without significant capital down.

The Raritan Valley Line doesn't offer the Midtown Direct service that Summit and Westfield enjoy, which is precisely why entry prices are lower. But there's an important development to track: NJ Transit has studied extending one-seat ride service to this corridor. If that happens, Cranford's discount to its neighbors narrows significantly.

Town government has been investing in downtown improvements and has been receptive to mixed-use development near the train station. More density near transit means more renters and more demand for existing housing stock.

## 3. Maplewood

**The Basics:**
- Commute to NYC Penn Station: 35-45 minutes (NJ Transit Midtown Direct)
- Median home price: ~$650,000
- Average 2BR rental: $2,400-$3,000/month
- School district rating: Columbia High School (shared with South Orange) is well-regarded

**Why Investors Should Care:**

Maplewood has one of the best commute-to-price ratios in Northern New Jersey. A 35-minute Midtown Direct ride at a median price roughly $300,000 below Summit is a compelling value proposition, and the market is starting to recognize it.

The town's diversity — economic, racial, and cultural — is a genuine asset, not just a talking point. It creates a broad and deep rental pool. You're not dependent on a single demographic. Young professionals, families, artists, remote workers who want more space — Maplewood attracts all of them.

The Maplewood Village area has developed into a legitimate dining and retail destination. Springfield Avenue has seen meaningful investment. These are the leading indicators of sustained appreciation that experienced investors look for.

For the multi-family investor, Maplewood's housing stock includes a solid inventory of two-family and three-family homes, many built in the early 1900s with good bones and architectural character. Value-add opportunities exist: updated kitchens and baths in these properties command premium rents from tenants who want the charm of an older home with modern finishes.

## 4. Montclair

**The Basics:**
- Commute to NYC Penn Station: 40-55 minutes (NJ Transit Montclair-Boonton Line)
- Median home price: ~$750,000
- Average 2BR rental: $2,600-$3,200/month
- School district rating: Good, with Montclair High School offering a well-known IB program

**Why Investors Should Care:**

Montclair is the most "discovered" town on this list, but I'd argue it's still undervalued relative to its long-term trajectory. The town has the cultural infrastructure — restaurants, theaters, galleries, nightlife — that most suburbs simply cannot replicate. That cultural gravity creates persistent rental demand from a demographic that values walkability and vibrancy over square footage.

The investment angle in Montclair is less about traditional residential rentals and more about the convergence of several trends: remote work driving demand from creative professionals, the town's established reputation attracting tenants willing to pay premium rents, and ongoing commercial development adding amenity value to surrounding residential areas.

Montclair's challenge is property taxes — they're high even by New Jersey standards. Factor them accurately into your underwriting. The numbers still work on well-located multi-family properties, but there's no room for sloppy math.

The upside: Montclair has proven remarkably resilient through market cycles. During the 2008-2012 correction, Montclair home values recovered faster than most of its peer towns. That kind of resilience matters in a buy-and-hold strategy.

## 5. Westfield

**The Basics:**
- Commute to NYC Penn Station: 50-60 minutes (NJ Transit Raritan Valley Line)
- Median home price: ~$850,000
- Average 2BR rental: $2,600-$3,200/month
- School district rating: Consistently ranked among New Jersey's best

**Why Investors Should Care:**

Westfield is a classic "buy the best school district you can afford" play. The school system is a demand engine that never turns off. Families will rent in Westfield at a premium specifically to access the schools, which gives landlords pricing power and low vacancy rates.

Downtown Westfield is one of the strongest traditional retail corridors in New Jersey. While many suburban downtowns have struggled with vacancy, Westfield has maintained high occupancy by attracting a mix of local businesses and strategic national tenants. A thriving downtown supports residential values — it's the amenity package that justifies premium pricing.

The investor opportunity is in the ring of smaller homes and multi-family properties within walking distance of the train station and downtown. These properties rent well to young families getting established and to professionals who want Westfield's quality of life without committing to a $1M+ single-family purchase.

Like Cranford, Westfield would benefit enormously from any future Midtown Direct expansion on the Raritan Valley Line. That's speculative upside on top of already-solid fundamentals.

## The Common Thread

What connects all five of these towns is a combination of factors that institutional investors and algorithms tend to overlook:

- **Structural supply constraints.** These are established towns with limited undeveloped land. You can't overbuild when there's nowhere to build.
- **NJ Transit access.** The commute to Manhattan is the demand engine. As long as New York is New York, these towns have a floor under their values.
- **School quality.** In New Jersey, school districts drive residential demand more than almost any other single factor.
- **Walkable downtowns.** Post-pandemic buyer and renter preferences have shifted decisively toward walkability. These towns already have it.

I'd encourage any investor considering Northern New Jersey to visit these towns in person. Walk the downtowns, ride the trains, talk to local property managers about vacancy rates and tenant quality. The spreadsheet only tells you part of the story.
