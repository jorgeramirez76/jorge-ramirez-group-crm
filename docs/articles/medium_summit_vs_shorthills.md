# Summit vs Short Hills: Where Should You Buy in 2026?

**A local agent's honest comparison of two of New Jersey's most sought-after towns**

If you're house-hunting in northern New Jersey's luxury corridor, you've almost certainly narrowed your search to a familiar shortlist. And somewhere near the top of that list, two names keep appearing: Summit and Short Hills.

Both towns sit in Union and Essex counties respectively, both offer top-tier school systems, and both deliver a fast commute into Manhattan. But the similarities mask real differences in lifestyle, housing stock, and value. After helping families navigate both markets for years, I want to share what the data sheets don't tell you.

---

## The Basics: Location and Commute

**Summit** is a small city in Union County, about 25 miles west of Midtown Manhattan. NJ Transit's Midtown Direct line gets you to Penn Station in roughly 50 minutes on an express train.

**Short Hills** is an unincorporated community within Millburn Township, Essex County. It sits on the same NJ Transit Midtown Direct line, with express trains reaching Penn Station in about 45 minutes.

That five-minute difference matters less than you'd think. What matters more is the station experience. Summit's downtown station is walkable from a large portion of the town, surrounded by restaurants and shops. Short Hills' station, while well-maintained, sits in a more residential setting. If you value a quick walk from the train to a coffee shop or dinner spot, Summit has the edge.

## Schools: Both Excellent, Different Strengths

Let me be clear: both districts consistently rank among New Jersey's best. You are not making a bad choice with either.

**Summit Public Schools** serve roughly 4,000 students across six schools. Summit High School regularly places well in statewide rankings, and the district has invested heavily in STEM programming and performing arts facilities.

**Millburn Township Public Schools** (which serve Short Hills) educate about 4,800 students and have long been considered one of the top districts in the state. Millburn High School is frequently cited in national rankings. The district is particularly well-regarded for its academic rigor and college placement outcomes.

For families with younger children, both districts offer strong elementary programs. Millburn's reputation often commands a premium in home pricing, which is worth factoring into your overall budget. Whether that premium translates into a meaningfully different educational outcome for your child is a conversation worth having with families already in each system.

## Neighborhoods and Housing Stock

This is where the two towns really diverge.

**Summit** offers a wider variety of housing. You'll find everything from walkable downtown condos and Victorians near the train to sprawling estates on the western side of town along streets like Kent Place Boulevard and Canoe Brook. First-time luxury buyers often find Summit more accessible. As of early 2026, median listing prices for single-family homes in Summit generally range from the high $800,000s into the low $2 millions, with premium properties going well above that.

**Short Hills** skews larger and more estate-like. The housing stock leans heavily toward Colonial and Tudor-style homes on generous lots. Streets like Old Short Hills Road and Hobart Avenue feature some of the most impressive residential properties in the state. Pricing tends to start higher than Summit, with median listings often beginning north of $1.2 million and climbing steeply in the most desirable sections.

*Note: Market pricing changes frequently. These are general ranges based on recent listing data and should not be taken as guarantees of current or future values. Always consult current MLS data for up-to-date information.*

## Downtown and Lifestyle

**Summit's downtown** is one of the most vibrant small-town centers in New Jersey. Springfield Avenue is lined with independent restaurants, boutiques, and services. You can walk to dinner, grab groceries, and meet friends for coffee all within a few blocks of the train station. The town has also invested in public gathering spaces, and there's a genuine sense of community in the downtown core.

**Short Hills** is quieter by design. The community is more residential in character, and daily errands often involve driving. The Short Hills Mall (officially The Mall at Short Hills) is a regional luxury shopping destination, but it serves a different purpose than a walkable downtown. Families in Short Hills tend to value privacy, lot size, and a more secluded feel.

Neither lifestyle is objectively better. But they are meaningfully different, and knowing which one fits your family's daily rhythm is critical.

## Taxes and Cost of Living

New Jersey property taxes are high across the board, and both towns reflect that reality. Summit and Short Hills/Millburn both carry effective tax rates that rank among the higher tiers in the state. However, because Short Hills homes tend to have higher assessed values, the absolute dollar amount of annual property taxes is often higher in Short Hills, even if the effective rate is comparable.

Factor in your total monthly carrying cost, including mortgage, taxes, insurance, and maintenance, when comparing these markets. A home that looks like a better deal on listing price can surprise you at the tax line.

## Community and Culture

**Summit** has a strong civic culture. The town hosts seasonal events, supports an active recreation department, and has a YMCA that serves as a community hub. There's an engaged parent community and a mix of young families, established residents, and professionals.

**Short Hills** draws families who often prioritize the school system above all else. The community is tight-knit but less event-driven than Summit's. Social life tends to center around school activities, sports leagues, and private clubs.

## So Which One Is Right for You?

I won't pretend there's a universal answer. Here's a simple framework:

**Consider Summit if you:**
- Want a walkable downtown with dining and nightlife options
- Prefer a broader range of price points and housing styles
- Value a strong sense of small-city community
- Want slightly more home for your dollar

**Consider Short Hills if you:**
- Prioritize lot size, privacy, and a more estate-like setting
- Want one of the highest-ranked school districts in the state
- Prefer a quieter, more residential daily experience
- Have a budget that comfortably supports the higher entry point

The best decision comes from spending time in both towns. Walk the streets on a Saturday morning. Drive the commute during rush hour. Visit the schools. Talk to residents. Real estate is personal, and no article can replace firsthand experience.
